# Selling at Nine

*I've made 943 serious mistakes in my life. I'm documenting every one of them so you don't have to make the same ones. My name is Lou Zerr. Here's another 2 of 943 (part 4 of 7).*

I sold at nine dollars a share on a Thursday in April 2001 from a payphone outside a Quiznos.

I sold at nine dollars a share on a Thursday in April 2001 from a payphone outside a Quiznos.

I'd like to tell you I sold from a Bloomberg terminal in a corner office with a view of a river. I sold from a payphone outside a Quiznos in a strip mall because my home internet was out, my work computer was being reimaged by IT, and my flip phone could not handle the brokerage website. I had driven to the Quiznos because there was a Kinko's two doors down where I could use a computer for nine dollars an hour, but the Kinko's computers were all taken by a group of people who appeared to be filing a class action lawsuit against something.

So: the payphone. I called my broker. His name was, let's say, Brent. Brent had a voice like a guy who had recently been promoted from cold-calling and was still excited about it.

Brent said, "Hey Lou, what can I do for you today."

I told him to sell all of it. Every share. Market order.

Brent paused. Brent said, "Lou, you sure? The stock's at, uh, $9.14 right now."

I said I was sure.

Brent said, "It's down a lot from where you, uh, where you might have wanted to —"

I told Brent I was aware of where I might have wanted to. I told Brent to sell.

Brent sold.

There's a moment, when you finally pull the trigger on a position that has been killing you for fourteen months, where you expect to feel relief. I had been promised this feeling by a personal finance book I'd been reading in secret, in the bathroom, like a teenager with a Playboy. The book said: the moment you accept the loss and act, you feel free.

I did not feel free. I felt like I had vomited into my own life and was now standing next to it.

I hung up the payphone. The receiver had a sticker on it that said FOR A GOOD TIME CALL with a number that had been crossed out and replaced with another number that had been crossed out. I stood there in the parking lot of the Quiznos for what felt like a long time but was probably four minutes. There was a woman loading groceries into a Dodge Caravan and her toddler was screaming. The toddler was screaming because the woman would not let the toddler hold a watermelon. The watermelon was bigger than the toddler. The toddler did not care. The toddler wanted the watermelon and could not have it and the toddler was expressing this in the only way available.

I understood the toddler.

I got in my Civic — the one with the perfectly functional transmission — and I sat there with the keys in the ignition and I did the math. I did the math the same way I had been doing it for months, calculating what different exit prices would mean, except this time the math was final.

At fifty-four dollars a share, back in early 2000, I had been almost a millionaire on paper. At forty-five I would have had enough for a great down payment on a house in a market that was about to triple over the next decade. At nine I had, after taxes and after paying off the credit card and after paying back my brother-in-law and after the IRA penalty, enough money to buy a used Subaru Outback.

Then I drove home and I had to tell my wife.

She was in the kitchen making spaghetti. The pineapple magnet was no longer on the fridge. It had migrated to a drawer about six months earlier, the way these things do, without ceremony. The fridge now had a magnet from the dry cleaner.

I told her I'd sold. I told her the number.

She stirred the spaghetti. She did not look at me. She said, "Okay."

She said it the way you say "okay" when there is nothing else to say and you have been waiting a long time to not say it.

The spaghetti was overcooked. We ate it anyway. The toddler in the parking lot, somewhere across town, was probably still screaming about the watermelon.

I did not buy a used Subaru Outback. I put what was left in a Vanguard fund and tried not to look at it for a year.

Next: the phone call to my brother-in-law. The lie. The transmission that never needed fixing.
