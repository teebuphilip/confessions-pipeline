# The Pineapple Magnet

*I've made 943 serious mistakes in my life. I'm documenting every one of them so you don't have to make the same ones. My name is Lou Zerr. Here's another 2 of 943 (part 1 of 7).*

I did the math on a Post-it note in the kitchen the morning the stock opened at fifty-four.

I did the math on a Post-it note in the kitchen the morning the stock opened at fifty-four.

I did it twice because I didn't believe it the first time. Then a third time because my hand was shaking and I'd written a six where a five should have been. The number at the bottom of the Post-it was bigger than any paycheck I'd ever earned. Not bigger than all of them combined—I wasn't that lucky—but bigger than any single one.

I stuck the Post-it to the fridge with a magnet shaped like a pineapple. The pineapple magnet had come from a hotel in Maui on a honeymoon we couldn't really afford. It had been holding up a coupon for Bertolli pasta sauce for about eight months. I moved the coupon. The pineapple was now load-bearing.

My wife came in wearing the blue robe with the hole in the elbow. She looked at the Post-it. She looked at me. She asked if we were going to be okay. I said we were going to be better than okay. I said we were going to be the kind of people that other people borrowed money from. She laughed the way you laugh at someone who's just told you they're going to be an astronaut.

I was thirty-one years old. I had been at the company for about eighteen months. It was a real company. We made software that did a specific, boring thing for hospitals. We had customers. We had contracts. We had a CFO who wore brown shoes with a black belt and never smiled.

The founder, a guy in his early forties who wore the same gray Patagonia vest every day like it was a priestly garment, had given me 500 shares when I joined. Not many by company standards, but enough to matter if things went well. He said he liked that I'd worked the weekend of the Charlotte launch. I'd worked the weekend of the Charlotte launch because my wife and I had been fighting and I didn't want to go home. He thought I had hustle. I had marital problems. The market doesn't care which one made you stay late.

Three weeks before the IPO, employees were allowed to buy additional shares at $2.50 each—the same price as the Series C round six months earlier. The amount I bought was not large by Silicon Valley standards but it was every dollar I had access to and several I didn't. I pulled $1,800 out of a Roth IRA and paid the penalty. I put $1,400 on a credit card with a 19.9% rate. And I borrowed four grand from my brother-in-law and told him it was for a transmission on my Civic. That bought me another 2,880 shares.

The Civic was fine. The transmission was fine. The Civic is now in a junkyard in Reno and the transmission outlived our marriage and the dog.

That morning, March of 1999, the pineapple magnet was holding up a Post-it that said, in my own handwriting: $182,520. My 500 original shares plus 2,880 purchased shares, times fifty-four dollars. I stood in the kitchen in my socks. The coffeemaker beeped. Somewhere on the radio a man was talking about how the new economy had rewritten the rules of valuation.

I believed him. I wanted to believe him. The pineapple believed him too.

I didn't sell a single share that day. I didn't need to. The lockup ran six months, and at the end of those six months the price would be even higher, and then I would sell, and then I would buy the house with the foyer, and then I would tell my brother-in-law the truth and we would laugh about the transmission.

None of those things happened in the order I planned.
