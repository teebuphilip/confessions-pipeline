# The IPO That Should Have Bought Me a House

*I've made 943 serious mistakes in my life. I'm documenting every one of them so you don't have to make the same ones. My name is Lou Zerr. This is mistake #2.*

---

The morning the stock opened at fifty-four dollars, I did the math on a Post-it note in the kitchen.

I did it twice because I didn't believe it the first time. Then I did it a third time because my hand was shaking and I'd written a six where a five should have been. The number at the bottom of the Post-it was bigger than every paycheck I'd ever earned added together. I stuck the Post-it to the fridge with a magnet shaped like a pineapple. My wife came in, looked at it, looked at me, and asked if we were going to be okay. I said we were going to be better than okay. I said we were going to be the people other people borrowed money from.

I was thirty-one. I had been at the company for eighteen months. The founder, a guy who wore the same gray Patagonia vest every day like it was a uniform, had given me a small grant of shares when I joined because he liked that I'd worked weekends on a launch. Not many. Enough. I'd also bought in at the initial stake price like an employee was supposed to, with money I didn't really have, money I'd pulled out of a Roth and a credit card and a loan from my brother-in-law that I told him was for a transmission.

And now it was March 2000 and the IPO had popped from thirteen to fifty-four and I was, on paper, the kind of person who buys houses with foyers.

The lockup expired in six months. I wrote the date on a calendar in red Sharpie. Every morning I'd check the stock price on Yahoo Finance before I checked my email. Fifty-one. Forty-eight. Fifty-two. Forty-six. I'd refresh and refresh and tell myself the dips were healthy. That's the word I used. Healthy. Like a stock was a kid eating vegetables.

A guy from finance, a quiet guy named — let's call him Dan — pulled me aside at a kitchenette in September. He said, "Lou, when the lockup lifts, sell half. Don't think. Don't pray. Sell half." I nodded. I nodded the way you nod when somebody tells you to wear sunscreen.

Lockup day came. The stock was at forty-five. I sat at my desk with the trade screen open. My finger was on the mouse. I had typed in the number of shares. All I had to do was click.

I didn't click.

I told myself I'd click at fifty. I told myself the analyst on CNBC said there was room to run. I told myself the company had real products, real customers, real revenue, and that was the difference between us and the Pets.com clowns. I told myself a lot of things in the forty-five seconds it took me to close the trade window without selling a single share.

That was the most expensive forty-five seconds of my life.

What happened next is its own story, and I'm not going to tell it here. I'll tell you that the pineapple magnet eventually came off the fridge. I'll tell you that I sold at nine. I'll tell you my brother-in-law still asks about that transmission.
