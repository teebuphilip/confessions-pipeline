# What I Should Have Done: The Rules I Follow Now

*I've made 943 serious mistakes in my life. I'm documenting every one of them so you don't have to make the same ones. My name is Lou Zerr. Here's another 2 of 943 (part 6 of 7).*

These aren't lessons. I don't do lessons. These are the rules I follow now because the version of me who didn't follow them is still paying.

These aren't lessons. I don't do lessons. These are the rules I follow now because the version of me who didn't follow them is still paying for the Quiznos payphone and the navy tie and the pineapple magnet.

If I had a time machine, I wouldn't go back and tell 31-year-old Lou anything inspirational. I'd hand him a printout of these and tell him to read it in the kitchen while the coffee was brewing.

When you have a windfall coming, decide how you'll sell before you can sell. Not the day of. Not the night before. Weeks before. The decision has to be made when the price isn't moving on a screen in front of you, because the screen is a casino and your brain on the screen is a tourist with a free drink coupon. Write the plan down on actual paper. Mine now lives in a manila folder in a filing cabinet I hate looking at. The folder has won me money. The folder has saved me money. The folder is dumber than I am and that is its superpower.

Sell half. I know how this sounds. Selling half feels like cowardice when the stock is going up and like genius when it's going down, and you will not know which one is coming. Selling half guarantees you cannot be 100% right and cannot be 100% wrong, and 100% right is not a thing a person like me is built to be. Dan from finance was right. He tapped his coffee mug twice with his ring finger and he was right.

Never invest borrowed money in something you would lie to the lender about. If you have to lie about what the money is for, the money should not be doing what you want to do with it. The lie is the tell. If 31-year-old Lou had had to tell Rick the truth — "Rick, I want $4,000 to buy pre-IPO shares in my company" — he would have heard Rick say what Rick would have said, which is, "Lou, that's not what loans from family are for." Rick would have been right. Rick is often right. This is part of why Rick is annoying.

If the company's fundamentals are sound and the stock is moving like a coin flip, the company is not what's moving the stock. This took me a decade to internalize. The stock market in March 2000 was not pricing hospital software companies. It was pricing a feeling. When the feeling changed, the price changed, and no amount of customer contracts in the southeast was going to override the feeling. Don't argue with the feeling. The feeling has more money than you.

Do not check the price more than once a day. Twice if there's news. The checking is not information. The checking is gambling without the chips. Eleven times a day is not analysis. Eleven times a day is the same brain chemistry as a slot machine, and slot machines do not love you.

Tell your spouse the truth in real time. Not the cleaned-up version on Sunday. The real version on Tuesday. My wife knew. She always knew. The thing I did not understand at thirty-one is that hiding the truth from her did not protect her from the truth — it just made her carry the truth and the hiding of the truth at the same time. Two things instead of one. That math is not in her favor and it's not in yours.

If a colleague with more scars than you offers unsolicited advice in a kitchenette, the advice is the gift. The kitchenette is a confessional and you are not the priest. Dan was not bragging. Dan was not posturing. Dan was a guy with a daughter at Penn State who had watched two previous IPOs eat people he liked, and he had decided he was going to try, exactly once, to keep it from happening to me. He tried once. He didn't try twice. That's how those guys work. If you get a Dan, listen to your Dan.

When the loss is real, take it. Don't average down on a dream. The stock at twenty-eight was not a discount on the stock at fifty-four. It was a different stock. It was a different company in a different market in a different country called The Future, and I was holding shares of a country called The Past.

The Subaru Outback would have been fine. I want to say that out loud. There was an alternate version of April 2001 where I sold at nine, bought the Outback in cash, drove it for fifteen years, and did not put the rest in a Vanguard fund I would mostly ignore. Both versions get me to roughly the same financial place today. The Outback version, though, has me driving a paid-off car through my thirties with no monthly note and a wife who maybe looks at me a little differently. The Outback version is a road not taken and I think about it more than I should.

That's the playbook. It will not make you rich. It will keep you from being the man at the payphone.
