# The Forty-Five Seconds

*I've made 943 serious mistakes in my life. I'm documenting every one of them so you don't have to make the same ones. My name is Lou Zerr. Here's another 2 of 943 (part 3 of 7).*

Lockup day was a Tuesday and I wore a tie to work for no reason.

Lockup day was a Tuesday and I wore a tie to work for no reason.

I didn't usually wear a tie. Nobody at the company wore a tie except the CFO and a guy in sales we called Powerpoint Greg. But that morning I knotted a navy tie with little white anchors on it and stood in front of the mirror and told my reflection that today was a serious day requiring serious decisions. My reflection looked like a guy who'd lost six pounds in three weeks from refreshing Yahoo Finance.

The stock had closed at $45.20 the day before.

Dan's plan, which I had spent six weeks talking myself out of, was to put in a limit order the night before for half my shares at market open and walk away. I had not done this. I had instead spent the previous evening reading a Motley Fool message board where a user named CoiledSpring2000 had laid out, with charts, why our stock was about to triple by Christmas.

I got to my desk at 8:14 a.m. Eastern. The market opened at 9:30. I had seventy-six minutes to do nothing. I did not do nothing. I opened the trade window. I typed in the number of shares that represented half my position. I stared at the field. I closed the window. I opened it again. I typed in a smaller number. I stared at it. I closed the window. I went to the kitchenette and Dan was not there, which felt like a sign, although a sign of what I couldn't say.

9:30 hit. The stock opened at $44.10. Down a dollar from the close. I told myself that was fine. Mornings were jittery. Wait for the afternoon. I had a thing I told myself called the eleven a.m. rule, which I had invented at approximately 9:32 a.m. that morning.

Eleven a.m. came. The stock was at $42.80. I told myself I'd sell at forty-five. Forty-five had been the close yesterday. Forty-five was a fair number. Forty-five was the floor.

It did not hit forty-five.

It hit 43.10 at noon and I went to lunch with a guy from product and ate a turkey sandwich I do not remember tasting. When I came back it was at 41.90. I told myself I'd sell at forty-three. Forty-three was a fair number. Forty-three was a respectable number. A new floor had been established mentally, retroactively, by me, in my chair, eating Altoids.

It did not hit forty-three.

By 3:15 it was at 40.50 and I had moved my mental floor to forty-one and was sweating through the navy tie. I opened the trade window one more time. I typed in the shares. My finger was on the mouse. The screen showed the order ready to go. All I had to do was click.

I did not click.

I sat there for what I now know was forty-five seconds because the screensaver kicked in at sixty and I had to move the mouse to keep it from going dark. I moved the mouse. The trade window was still there. The shares were still typed in. The price had ticked down to 40.30. I told myself I would not sell at a price that felt like surrender. I told myself selling now was capitulation. I told myself I had not come this far to walk away at a loss.

I closed the trade window.

The stock closed that day at 39.80. I went home and didn't tell my wife I hadn't sold. I told her the market was choppy and I was going to wait for a better print. She looked at me with a mixture of concern and disappointment that I'd seen before.

Next month it was at twenty-eight. Next quarter, fifteen. I held. I held the way a man holds a steering wheel as the car goes over the cliff.
