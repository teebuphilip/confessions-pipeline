# The Tail: What It Still Costs Me

*I've made 943 serious mistakes in my life. I'm documenting every one of them so you don't have to make the same ones. My name is Lou Zerr. Here's another 2 of 943 (part 7 of 7).*

I drive past the house I would have bought about once a month.

I drive past the house I would have bought about once a month.

I don't do it on purpose. It's on a road I take to get to a hardware store I like, a hardware store with an old guy named — call him Mel — who knows where every weird gasket is. The house is on the left, third from the corner, brick with a foyer that has one of those half-circle windows above the front door. I'd seen it on a real estate flyer in March 2000 when I was running the numbers and thinking about foyers. It was listed at $340,000. Today it'd go for north of a million and a half, easy. I checked Zillow once. I do not check Zillow now.

The house has been owned by the same family since 2001. I can tell because the basketball hoop in the driveway has aged with the kids. In the early 2000s it was a kid-height hoop. By 2008 it was full height. By 2015 there were two cars in the driveway with college parking stickers. Now there's a car seat in the back of a Honda Pilot and I assume one of the kids has had a kid. The family has done their life in that house. It has been the backdrop of a real one.

That's the tail. That's what it still costs me. Not the money. The money I've made back, mostly, in the boring way, over twenty years of 401(k) contributions and a few smart-by-accident decisions. The money is a wash. The money is not the thing.

The thing is the house. The thing is the version of my life that had a foyer.

In that version, my wife and I moved in during the summer of 2001. In that version, we had two kids instead of one. In that version, the second kid happened because there was a room for the second kid, and rooms have a way of summoning the people who go in them. In that version, my marriage maybe survives, because some of what killed it was the slow grind of feeling like we were always almost about to have the life we'd planned, instead of just having it. In that version, the pineapple magnet is still on the fridge in a kitchen that is mine.

I know this is a fantasy. I know the alternate-Lou with the foyer probably found other ways to lose. There's a version of me with the house and a different disaster — a worse marriage, a sicker kid, a heart attack at forty-eight. The alternate lives aren't necessarily better. I know this intellectually. The hardware store route does not care what I know intellectually.

There are smaller tails too. I notice them in specific moments.

When Rick brings up the transmission at a family gathering, my whole body does the thing it does. Shoulders up an inch. Smile that is not a smile. I have learned to breathe through it. I have not learned to make it not happen.

When a coworker tells me about an opportunity — a friend's startup, a side investment, anything with the word "pre" in front of it — I get a feeling in my stomach that is part nausea and part hunger. I have made peace with not acting on the hunger. I have not made peace with the nausea. The nausea is a permanent tenant.

When my daughter, who is now older than I was when all this happened, asks me for advice about money, I have to physically stop myself from over-explaining. She asked me once about contributing to a Roth and I talked for forty-five minutes. She finally said, "Dad. I asked a yes-or-no question." I said yes. I should have said yes thirty-five minutes earlier. The over-explaining is a tail. The over-explaining is me trying to vaccinate her with a needle that's too big.

And there's the dreams. I still have, maybe four times a year, a dream about a trade window. I am sitting in front of a screen. The shares are typed in. The price is moving. My finger is on the mouse. I cannot click. I wake up before I click. I have had this dream in 2003, 2008, 2014, last March, and twice in October. The dream is not subtle. The dream has never been subtle. The dream is my brain still asking why.

I don't have a good answer for the dream. I have the rules. I have the folder in the filing cabinet. I have a wife — a different one, a second one, a better one for me at this stage — who knows the whole story and doesn't make jokes about it. I have a daughter who calls. I have a hardware store guy named Mel. I have a Civic — a different Civic — that genuinely did need a transmission in 2019 and I paid for it in cash and I thought about Rick when I signed the receipt.

The tail is that some mistakes don't end. They taper. They get smaller. They become a thing you drive past once a month on the way to a hardware store, a thing you nod at, a thing you do not stop the car for.

The pineapple magnet, by the way, is in a drawer in my kitchen. I moved it from house to house. I do not put it on the fridge. I do not throw it away.

It lives in the drawer. It is mine. It cost me a foyer.

— Lou
