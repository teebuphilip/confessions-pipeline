# Year One: I Already Knew

I knew by the honeymoon.

Not in some dramatic, throw-the-ring-in-the-ocean way. Quieter than that. We were sitting at a restaurant in some town I can't remember the name of anymore, and there was a long pause in the conversation, and I realized neither of us was uncomfortable with it. Not in the good way. In the way where two strangers on a bus don't feel awkward because they don't expect anything from each other.

That was my marriage. Two people on a bus.

I came home and didn't tell anybody. Who would I tell? My parents had spent six figures on the wedding. My friends had flown in. Her family had taken me into theirs with the specific intensity of people who believe their daughter has just been saved from spinsterhood, which, in their math, was a fate worse than cancer.

So I did what losers do. I waited. I told myself, give it a year. Then, give it two. Then, the kid will change things — and we did have a kid, and the kid did not change things, because kids don't fix marriages, they just give the marriage more weight to drag around.

The first time I really thought about leaving, I was thirty-one. I sat in my car in a parking lot for an hour. I had a number in my head — what it would cost. The lawyer. The split. Moving out. Two households. The shame tax, which is invisible but real, the discount you take on your own life because you're too embarrassed to be seen failing.

I added it up and the number scared me. So I drove home.

That parking lot, by the way, is the most expensive real estate I've ever occupied. I paid for it with the next nineteen years.

We were polite to each other. That's the part nobody tells you about bad marriages — they're not always loud. Mine was a library. We whispered through it. We were excellent roommates. We coordinated schedules. We were, by the standards of anyone watching from the outside, fine.

Fine is the word that ate my thirties and forties.

— Lou
