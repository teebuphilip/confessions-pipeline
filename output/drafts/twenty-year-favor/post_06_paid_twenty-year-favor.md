# What I Should Have Done: The Actual Playbook

If I could send a letter to twenty-eight-year-old me, it would not be inspirational. It would be a checklist.

Here it is. Use it for yourself. Use it for someone you love who's about to make my mistake.

1. When family pressure shows up, name it out loud, to them. Say the words: I feel pressured. Watch what they do. People who love you will back off. People who are using love as leverage will double down. You will know within ten minutes which kind you're dealing with.

2. Never get married inside a twelve-month window of any major family event — a death, an illness, a milestone birthday. Grief and guilt distort the timeline. If the wedding can't survive a one-year delay, it can't survive a marriage.

3. Have one conversation with your intended, alone, no phones, where you both answer this question: if our families disappeared tomorrow, would we still be doing this? If either of you hesitates, you have your answer. We hesitated. We both hesitated. We pretended we hadn't.

4. Talk to a therapist before the wedding. Not couples counseling. Individual. One session. Tell them everything. They will not tell you what to do. They will ask questions you have been avoiding. The cost is two hundred dollars. The cost of skipping it, in my case, was twenty years.

5. If you're already married and you know it's wrong, do not make the spreadsheet. The spreadsheet is a trap. Instead, do this: write down what your life will look like in ten years if nothing changes. Not finances. Daily life. Mornings, evenings, weekends. Read it out loud. If you can't stand to hear it, you have your answer.

6. Get a lawyer consultation. Most are free or cheap. You are not committing to anything. You are gathering information. The boogeyman in your head is always bigger than the actual numbers.

7. Tell one person. Just one. Pick someone who is not invested in your marriage continuing. Not your mother. Not her best friend. Someone neutral. You need a witness. Secrets metastasize. Witnessed truth doesn't.

8. Separate your money. Not as a hostile act. As a sanity act. Have your own account. Have your own credit. Know your own numbers. The day you can answer the question what would my life cost alone is the day you stop being financially trapped, even if you choose to stay.

9. Stop using the kid as a reason. The kid sees everything. The kid is learning what to expect from love by watching you. If you wouldn't want your kid in your marriage twenty years from now, you don't get to use the kid as the reason you're in yours.

10. The number you are afraid of — the cost of leaving — write it down. Then write down the cost of staying for another decade. Use real units. Years. Mornings. Versions of yourself you will never get to meet. Compare honestly.

I ran from honest comparison for twenty years. Don't.

— Lou
