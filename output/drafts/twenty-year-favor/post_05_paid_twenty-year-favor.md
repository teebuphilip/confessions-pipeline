# The Eighteen-Month Crawl

Knowing you have to leave and actually leaving are separated by an ocean.

I crossed it in eighteen months. Slowly. Stupidly. With many detours.

First thing I did was nothing. For three months. I sat with the bathroom-floor decision and I didn't act on it because I was scared, and because I had spent twenty years training myself not to act, and you don't undo twenty years of training in a weekend.

Then I did the second-worst thing, which is I tried to fix it. I suggested counseling. I read books. I tried to manufacture intimacy in a marriage that had been a contract from day one. This is what reasonable people do, and reasonable people are sometimes wrong. You can't repair a building that was built without a foundation. You can repaint it. We repainted for about four months. It looked better. It was the same building.

Then I did the third-worst thing, which is I told one friend. Just one. I needed a witness. I needed someone outside my head to hear it and not flinch. He didn't flinch. He said something I'll never forget. He said, Lou, you've been describing this marriage to me for fifteen years like you're describing the weather. Nobody describes a marriage they're in like the weather.

Then I started moving money. Not hiding it — I want to be clear, I didn't do anything sneaky with assets. I just started understanding our finances for the first time as my finances and her finances, instead of our finances. I talked to a lawyer, just a consultation. I talked to a financial planner. I learned what the actual cost of leaving was, versus the imaginary cost the old spreadsheet had been screaming about.

The actual cost was less than I thought. It always is. Fear inflates every number it touches.

Then I told her. That conversation gets its own treatment somewhere else, but the short version: she wasn't surprised. She had her own bathroom floor. She had been waiting too. We had both been waiting, for twenty years, for the other one to be brave first. We had been each other's hostages, and neither of us had been holding the gun.

The divorce took nine months after that. It was not nasty. It was sad in the way that cleaning out a house after someone dies is sad — there's no enemy, just a pile of life that has to be sorted.

I moved into a one-bedroom apartment with bad lighting and a view of a parking lot. I sat on the floor the first night because I didn't have a couch yet. And I felt something I had not felt in two decades.

Quiet. The good kind.

— Lou
