# I Stayed Married for Twenty Years Because I Was Too Cheap to Leave

My aunt cried at the engagement party. Not happy tears. Relieved tears. The kind that said: finally, someone is taking this problem off our hands.

I was the problem. Twenty-eight years old, still single, still making my parents nervous at dinner parties when their friends asked about grandchildren. My future wife was the solution. Or she was supposed to be.

We weren't in love. I need to say that upfront because it matters. We weren't even particularly in like. We were two people who had been seated next to each other at enough family functions that the adults in the room decided the universe was speaking. The universe apparently sounded like my Aunt Marlene sobbing into a napkin.

I said yes the way you sign a lease on an apartment you've already half-moved into. The deposit was gone. Walking away felt expensive.

It wasn't. Walking away is never more expensive than staying. I just didn't have the math yet.

---

I knew by the honeymoon.

We were sitting at a restaurant in some town I can't remember anymore. There was a long pause in the conversation, and I realized neither of us was uncomfortable with it. Not in the good way. In the way two strangers on a bus don't feel awkward because they don't expect anything from each other.

That was my marriage. Two people on a bus. For twenty years.

I came home and didn't tell anybody. Who would I tell? My parents had spent six figures on the wedding. Her family had taken me in with the intensity of people who believe their daughter has just been saved from spinsterhood. Everyone was so goddamn happy.

So I did what losers do. I waited. I told myself, give it a year. Then give it two. Then the kid will change things.

We had a kid. The kid did not change things. Kids don't fix marriages. They just give the marriage more weight to drag around.

---

Around year five, I made a spreadsheet.

I'm not a spreadsheet guy. I can barely operate a microwave. But I made this one because I wanted to prove to myself, mathematically, that I couldn't afford to leave.

And I did. I proved it. Columns. Numbers. A bottom line that said: stay.

What the spreadsheet didn't have a column for: the weight in my chest every morning. The friendships I let die. The drinking that crept up one glass at a time. The version of me that was supposed to exist by forty and never showed up. The affair. The lying that came with it.

Money is measurable. Misery isn't. So when you put them on the same page, money wins every time. You're comparing a number to a feeling, and the feeling always loses on paper.

But you don't live on paper. You live in your body. And my body knew what the spreadsheet was lying about.

I ran that spreadsheet probably forty times over fifteen years. I treated it like a religious document. Every time, the answer came back the same. Stay. Stay. Stay.

You know what that spreadsheet really was? Permission. A permission slip I wrote to myself to keep being a coward.

---

At forty-seven, I ended up on a bathroom floor.

I didn't plan it. I came home early from a work trip. She was watching television. She said hi. I said hi back. I went upstairs. I sat on the tile next to the bath mat we'd picked out together at some store I can't remember, and I cried. Not because I was sad. Because I was tired. The kind of tired where you suddenly understand why people do extreme things.

I asked myself one question that night: if I die at seventy-five, how many years do I have left?

Twenty-eight.

How many of them am I willing to spend on this Tuesday, repeated?

The answer changed everything.

---

I'm telling the whole story. The bottom. The eighteen months it took to crawl out. The coward moves. The night I finally told her. What I should have done at twenty-eight. And the bill that keeps arriving, seven years after the divorce, that I'll be paying for the rest of my life.

The first three parts are free. The rest is on my Substack, where I'm documenting all 943 mistakes I've made so you don't have to make them too.
