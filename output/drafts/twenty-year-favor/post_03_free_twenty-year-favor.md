# The Spreadsheet That Ate My Life

Around year five, I made a spreadsheet.

I want you to understand something. I am not a spreadsheet guy. I can barely operate a microwave. But I made this one because I wanted to prove to myself, mathematically, that I couldn't afford to leave.

And I did. I proved it. I sat there at the kitchen table at two in the morning and I had columns and I had numbers and I had a bottom line and the bottom line said: stay.

What the spreadsheet did not have a column for:

The weight in my chest every morning.
The way I started drinking a little more each year, like a frog in a pot.
The friendships I let die because bringing my wife was awkward and going alone was awkwarder.
The version of me that was supposed to exist by forty and never showed up.
The affair I almost had, then the one I did have, then the lying that came with it, which is its own full-time job.
The kid, who was not stupid, and who learned what marriage looks like by watching mine.

None of that was on the spreadsheet. The spreadsheet only knew about the mortgage and the 401k and the cost of two apartments versus one house.

Here's the thing about money as a reason to stay. Money is measurable. Misery isn't. So when you put them on the same page, money wins every time, because it's the only one with numbers next to it. You're comparing a real thing to a feeling, and the feeling always loses on paper.

But you don't live on paper. You live in your body. And my body knew, every single day, what the spreadsheet was lying about.

I ran that spreadsheet probably forty times over the next fifteen years. I updated it. I refined it. I treated it like a religious document. Every time the answer came back the same. Stay. Stay. Stay.

You know what the spreadsheet was? It was permission. It wasn't analysis. It was a permission slip I wrote to myself to keep being a coward.

That's the free part of the story. The setup. The disaster is in the rest, and the rest is where the real damage lives — the stuff I haven't told anyone, including, for a long time, myself.

— Lou
