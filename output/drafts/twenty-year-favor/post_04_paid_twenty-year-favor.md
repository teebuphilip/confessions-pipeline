# The Bathroom Floor at Forty-Seven

I want to tell you exactly what the bottom looked like, because nobody ever describes it accurately.

The bottom is not dramatic. The bottom is boring. The bottom is a Tuesday.

It was a Tuesday in November. I had come home from a work trip a day early. I don't know why. I think part of me wanted to catch her doing something — not because I cared romantically, but because I wanted a reason. I wanted permission, the same permission the spreadsheet had been denying me. Catch her cheating and the whole thing is justified. Easy exit. No villain in the mirror.

She wasn't doing anything. She was watching television. She looked up, said hi, didn't ask why I was home early. I said hi back. I went upstairs. I sat on the bathroom floor with the door closed and I cried, and I want to be specific about this because men don't talk about it — I cried not because I was sad but because I was tired. Bone-tired. The kind of tired where you understand, suddenly, why people do extreme things. Not because they want to. Because they can't carry it for one more day.

I was forty-seven. I had been married for nineteen years. I had stayed for the kid, for the money, for my parents, for her parents, for the version of me that needed to look responsible at Thanksgiving. I had not stayed for her, and she had not stayed for me, and we both knew it, and we had both agreed in some unspoken way to never say it out loud.

That night I did new math. Not on a spreadsheet. On a notepad. I asked myself one question: if I die at seventy-five, which is a generous estimate for a guy who lives like me, how many years do I have left?

Twenty-eight.

And how many of those am I willing to spend on this Tuesday, repeated?

Zero.

That was the first honest number I had run in two decades.

I didn't leave that night. I'm not going to lie to you. The crawl back from the bottom takes longer than the fall. But something cracked open on that bathroom floor and it never closed again. The lie I had been telling myself — that staying was responsible — died there, on the tile, next to the bath mat my wife and I had picked out together at a store I cannot remember.

I got up. I washed my face. I went downstairs. I said, want some tea. She said sure. We drank tea. And inside my head, for the first time in nineteen years, the spreadsheet was on fire.
