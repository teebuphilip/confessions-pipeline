# The Tail: The Bill That Keeps Arriving

I've been divorced for seven years. I thought leaving was the end of the cost. It was the down payment.

Here's what nobody tells you about staying somewhere for twenty years that you should have left.

You don't get the years back. Obviously. Everyone knows that. That's the cliché. But the cliché doesn't capture the texture of it.

The texture is this: I am fifty-five. I am dating again, which is its own circle of hell, but that's not the point. The point is, when I meet someone now, I am meeting them with twenty years of atrophied muscle. I forgot how to be in love. I forgot how to want. I spent two decades training myself out of those feelings because they were dangerous in a marriage where they had no home. Now I'm trying to switch them back on and the wiring is corroded.

I watch men my age who married well at twenty-five and they have a fluency I don't have. They know how to fight and recover. They know how to want their partner without it feeling like a crisis. I'm learning all of it from scratch at fifty-five. I am a beginner at intimacy. That is the tail.

My kid. My kid is twenty-three now. We have a fine relationship. Fine, that word again. But there is a thing we don't talk about, which is that he watched two people pretend for his entire childhood. He has told me, in not so many words, that he doesn't know what real looks like. He's careful with women in a way that is not healthy carefulness, it is the carefulness of someone who learned that love is a performance and the performance has to be maintained or everything collapses. I gave him that. I cannot un-give it. I can only sit with it.

My parents are dead now. Both of them. They died proud of me, in the sense that I had stayed married, which was the metric they cared about. I never told them the truth about the marriage. I let them die inside the lie. I tell myself that was a kindness. Some days I believe it. Some days I think I robbed all three of us of one honest conversation, and that is a theft I cannot return.

The money. Here's the kicker on the money, the original reason I stayed. I am fine. I am not rich, but I am fine. The financial apocalypse I spent twenty years avoiding never would have happened. I ran the numbers, on the other side, with a real planner. If I had left at thirty, I would have been more comfortable today than I am now. Staying for the money cost me money. That's the punchline. The thing I sold my life for, I didn't even get.

And the smallest, sharpest one, the one that wakes me up sometimes: there was a version of me that existed at twenty-eight, with possibilities branching out in front of him like a tree. That guy is gone. He didn't get to live. I killed him slowly over twenty years to keep a dinner table quiet. I think about him sometimes. I owe him an apology I can't deliver.

Don't stay because of money. Don't stay because of family. Don't stay because of inertia, which is just the other three wearing a disguise.

The bill keeps arriving. I'm still paying it. I'll be paying it the rest of my life.

You don't have to.
