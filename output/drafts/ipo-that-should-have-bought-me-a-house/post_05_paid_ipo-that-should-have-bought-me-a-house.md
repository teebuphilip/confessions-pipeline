# The Transmission Call

*I've made 943 serious mistakes in my life. I'm documenting every one of them so you don't have to make the same ones. My name is Lou Zerr. Here's another 2 of 943 (part 5 of 7).*

My brother-in-law's name is — let's call him Rick — and I owed him four thousand dollars and a story.

My brother-in-law's name is — let's call him Rick — and I owed him four thousand dollars and a story.

The four thousand I had paid back in March 2000, two weeks after the IPO popped, with a check that had a memo line reading "TRANSMISSION — THANKS BRO." Rick had laughed when he got it. He had called me and said, "Civic running smooth?" and I had said, "Like a dream," and we had moved on.

The story was harder.

I did not tell Rick the truth for a long time. Not in 2000, when I should have. Not in 2001, when I sold at nine and the not-telling became its own ongoing crime. Not in 2003, at his daughter's first birthday, when he made a joke about how I'd been driving the same Civic for four years and clearly his loan had bought me eternal automotive life. Not at most of the Thanksgivings between 2000 and 2004, when he kept the joke alive like a cactus, watering it once a year with just enough laughter to keep it from dying.

The cowardice was layered. I want you to see the layers.

Layer one: I did not tell him at the start because I was embarrassed to ask for money to gamble with. The transmission lie was easier. A transmission was a problem a man could have. A Roth IRA penalty plus a credit card plus a stock buy was a problem a different kind of man had, and I wasn't ready to be that man in front of Rick.

Layer two: I did not tell him after the IPO popped because telling him then would have meant telling him I'd made a lot of money on something he'd unknowingly funded. He would have been happy for me. He would also, somewhere in his head, have run the math on what his share might have been if I'd cut him in. I didn't want him to run that math. So I kept quiet.

Layer three: I did not tell him after I sold at nine because by then the lie had aged into a fixture. Telling him in 2001 would have meant explaining not just the lie but the year of additional lying. And the loss. And the fact that I'd lost not just my own money but, in a sense, his goodwill, which he didn't even know he'd given.

Layer four, and this is the worst one: I started pulling back from him. Not dramatically. Just enough. I skipped two cookouts in 2002 with vague excuses. I let some of his calls go to voicemail and returned them at hours I knew he wouldn't pick up. At family holidays, I was present but distant. My wife noticed. She didn't push.

Layer five: when I finally told him, in 2021, at a Thanksgiving with two glasses of wine in me and four in him, I told him in the worst possible way. I told him while passing the green beans. I said, "Rick, that loan in '99 wasn't for the transmission." He looked at me. I said, "It was for a stock buy. The company IPO'd. I made some money. I lost most of it. I paid you back fine, but I lied about what it was for, and I'm sorry."

There was a silence at the table that had a shape. My niece, now grown, looked at her phone. My mother-in-law looked at her plate. My wife looked at me with an expression I'd seen exactly once before, the night I sold at nine, and I understood then that she had known. Of course she had known. She had known for twenty-two years and had let me carry it alone because she had been waiting for me to put it down myself.

Rick chewed. Rick swallowed. Rick said, "Huh."

Then Rick said, "So what'd you make?"

I told him the number at the peak. I told him the number when I sold.

Rick whistled. Rick said, "Lou, you absolute donkey."

And that should have been the end of it. It was not the end of it. The transmission joke, which had been a low hum for two decades, became a feature. Rick now mentions it at every family gathering. He introduces me to strangers as "the guy who lied about a transmission." He brings it up in front of waiters. He brought it up at my niece's college graduation last spring. He will bring it up this weekend at a lake house, and I am — and this is the part I'm not proud of — planning what I'm going to say back.

Next: the rules. The ones I follow now. The ones I should have followed then.
